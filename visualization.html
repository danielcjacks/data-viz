Income disparity in Australia

Australia's GDP is the 13th highest out of 190 countries The top 20% of Australians have 90 times more wealth than the bottom 20% Low income can have impacts on outcomes such as health, education and quality of life

Regional population


Australia's population is not evenly distributed.

  • Most Australians live in the cooler, less arid coastal regions
  • Australia's five largest cities hold 65% of the population but take up only 0.6% of the total area

Regional income


Urban regions are defined by the European Commission as settlements with:

  • 5,000 inhabitants
  • 300 people per square km


Use the controls below to highlight urban regions.

Many of the regions with very high average incomes in Australia are in large cities. For example, Sydney is Australia's largest city with over 5.3M people. The average annual family income in urban Sydney is 140k, while the national average is 120k. That means the average in Sydney is 16% higher than the national average.

Income distribution


The income vs population density chart on the right highlights some important features:

  • Most rural regions have a similar average income
  • There is a sharp increase in income in urban regions
  • Rural regions are in a wider band since their low population totals cause increased statistical uncertainty

Industry distribution


Looking at the largest industry in each region can help understand the income disparity:

  • Higher paying jobs such as utility services or scientific and technical services are more common in more populated regions
  • Rural areas have alot of agriculture jobs, which are the worst paying jobs
  • Rural jobs are more volatile, for example:
    • Agriculture is dependent on the season
    • Mining is dependent on the demand from international markets
